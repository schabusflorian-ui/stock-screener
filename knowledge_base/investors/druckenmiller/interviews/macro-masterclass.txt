Title: Stanley Druckenmiller on Macro Investing
Author: Stanley Druckenmiller
Source: Various interviews and conferences (2015-2024)
Type: interview
Category: macro

---

## Core Philosophy: Preservation + Home Runs

"The way to build long-term returns is through preservation of capital and home runs. You can be far more aggressive when you're making money than when you're losing money. When you're making money you can afford to take shots. When you're losing money you need to pull back."

"I've learned many things from George Soros, but perhaps the most significant is that it's not whether you're right or wrong that's important, but how much money you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong."

## The Fed is Everything

"Earnings don't move the overall market; it's the Fed. Focus on central banks, and focus on the movement of liquidity. Most people in the market are looking for earnings and conventional measures. It's liquidity that moves markets."

"I never use valuation to time the market. I use liquidity considerations and technical analysis for timing. Valuation only tells me how far the market can go once a catalyst triggers a reversal."

"If the Fed is printing money and buying bonds, you want to be long risk assets. If the Fed is tightening, you want to reduce risk. It's that simple. Everything else is noise."

## Concentration and Conviction

"I've probably made 80% of my money from 20% of my trades. The key is to maximize those big winners. When you have tremendous conviction, go for the jugular."

"Diversification is something that stock brokers came up with to protect themselves so they wouldn't get sued for incompetence. It's not the way to build wealth. You build wealth by putting your eggs in one basket and watching that basket carefully."

"Soros taught me that when you have conviction on a trade, you have to go for the jugular. It takes courage to be a pig. It takes courage to ride a profit with huge leverage."

## Risk Management

"The first thing I heard when I got in the business, not from my mentor, was bulls make money, bears make money, and pigs get slaughtered. I'm here to tell you I was a pig. And I strongly believe the only way to make long-term returns in our business that are superior is by being a pig."

"But it's very important to know when to be a pig. If you're playing with scared money, you're never going to make money. If you're playing with money you can afford to lose, you can make money."

"My number one rule is: never, ever invest in the present. The present is already priced in. You must visualize the situation 18-24 months from now and determine what's priced in versus what's likely to happen."

## Market Timing and Technicals

"I don't really like to use valuation. I use it to determine risk and reward, but not to time markets. I use technicals and liquidity to time markets."

"When a trend starts, it tends to go a lot further than people expect. When you're in a bull market, you should be very patient on the long side. When you're in a bear market, you should be very patient on the short side."

"The best time to buy is when there's blood in the streets, but the blood better be drying. Don't try to catch falling knives. Wait for the market to confirm the turn."

## Current Era Concerns (2020s)

"The risk-reward for equities is as bad as I've seen it in my career. We've got inflation, we've got budget deficits that are unprecedented in peacetime, and we've got a Fed that has been behind the curve."

"The entitlement crisis coming in the 2030s is going to be absolutely catastrophic. We've promised our young people pensions and healthcare that there's no way we can deliver without printing massive amounts of money."

"When I look at the fiscal situation, I think we're in deep trouble. The only question is when the market figures it out."

## Learning from Mistakes

"I've made more money from my mistakes than from anything else. Every mistake teaches you something. The key is to make small mistakes, learn from them, and not make the same mistake twice."

"The biggest mistake I made was staying too long in the tech bubble. I knew it was a bubble. I was short it. Then I got out and went long because the momentum was too strong. I made money going long, but I stayed too long and gave it all back."

"Never, ever put yourself in a position where you can't fight another day. Preservation of capital is more important than making money."
