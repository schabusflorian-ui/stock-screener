Title: Sample Berkshire Letter - Wisdom Compilation
Source: Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letters
Author: Warren Buffett
Date: 2023

---

CHAIRMAN'S LETTER

To the Shareholders of Berkshire Hathaway Inc.:

Our goal is simple: to find wonderful businesses at fair prices, not fair businesses at wonderful prices. The key characteristics we seek are durable competitive advantages, honest and able management, and reasonable valuations.

THE IMPORTANCE OF MARGIN OF SAFETY

When we buy stocks, we think like a business owner would think about buying the whole company. We want a margin of safety - a discount from intrinsic value that protects us from permanent loss of capital.

The margin of safety concept is the cornerstone of investment success. If you pay too much for a stock, even if the business is wonderful, you may still earn poor returns. The price you pay determines your return.

COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES AND MOATS

We look for businesses with economic moats - sustainable competitive advantages that protect the business from competition. These moats can come from:

1. Brand power and customer loyalty
2. Network effects where the product becomes more valuable as more people use it
3. Switching costs that make it expensive for customers to change
4. Cost advantages from economies of scale
5. Regulatory advantages or patents

A business without a moat is like a castle without walls. Eventually, competition will erode its profits. We want businesses where the moat is widening, not shrinking.

MANAGEMENT QUALITY AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

The most important job of a CEO is capital allocation. How management deploys the cash the business generates determines long-term shareholder returns. We look for managers who:

- Think like owners, not employees
- Have significant personal investment in the business
- Are honest and transparent with shareholders
- Make rational capital allocation decisions
- Avoid empire building and focus on returns

We've seen too many managers destroy value through ill-conceived acquisitions or share buybacks at inflated prices.

LONG-TERM THINKING

The stock market is designed to transfer money from the active to the patient. Short-term thinking is the enemy of good investing. We don't care about quarterly earnings - we care about where the business will be in 10 or 20 years.

Time is the friend of the wonderful business and the enemy of the mediocre. A business that can compound at high rates of return for many years will create enormous wealth for its owners.

CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE

We stay within our circle of competence. We know what we understand and what we don't. It's not important how big your circle is - what matters is knowing where the boundaries are.

We've passed on many opportunities because we didn't understand the business well enough. Missing out on potential gains is far less painful than suffering losses from investments we didn't understand.

ON RISK

Risk comes from not knowing what you're doing. The best risk management is buying good businesses at good prices and holding them for the long term. Diversification may protect against ignorance, but it cannot replace knowledge.

Charlie and I define risk as the probability of permanent capital loss, not volatility. A stock that drops 50% is not twice as risky - if the business is sound, it may actually be less risky because you can buy more at a lower price.
